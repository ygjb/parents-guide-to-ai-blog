Part 7 - School AI vs. Home AI: What Every VSB Parent Should Understand

By Yvan Boily

Tags: school VSB Copilot privacy policy

Part 7 of 12 in the Try It Yourself series — practical AI guidance for parents at blog.parentsguidetoai.ca

In May 2026, the Vancouver School Board announced that eligible secondary students would have access to Microsoft Copilot 13+ through school accounts.1 If your child is a VSB secondary student, they may already be using it — or will be soon.

Here's the thing: Copilot 13+ and the Copilot you can download on your phone are not the same product. They share the same name and the same underlying technology, but they operate under very different rules.

This post explains those differences — so you know what protections exist at school, what gaps exist at home, and what questions are worth asking.

It's also important to make a disclaimer here - my understanding of the differences is based on publicly available documentation from Microsoft, and from the VSB announcements. I don't have an inside track on the actual terms and conditions, or what agreements that VSB and Microsoft have in place besides what has been published. If you learn about or find mistakes in this page, I would appreciate you reporting them to me by opening an issue here!

What Copilot 13+ includes that the public version doesn't

Section 2 of the parent's guide lists the protections that the VSB's school version of Copilot may include. This is worth reading carefully, because it explains why the school made the choice it made — and why "it's just Copilot" isn't a complete picture.

According to the VSB, the school version includes:

  • School-managed accounts — created and controlled by the school district, not by your child personally
  • Commercial data protection — conversations may not be used to train public AI models
  • Content filtering — designed to reduce exposure to harmful material
  • Encrypted data handling
  • No use of student prompts to train public AI models
  • Integration with school digital citizenship expectations2

Compare that to the public version of Copilot, which you might access at copilot.microsoft.com with a personal Microsoft account. The public version is useful — we recommended it in Post 1 as an easy starting point. But it doesn't come with those same protections by default.

This matters because your child may not distinguish between "the AI I use at school" and "the AI I can use at home." From their perspective, it looks like the same thing. But from a data protection standpoint, it isn't.

Why "school-approved" doesn't automatically mean "home-approved"

The parent's guide offers a principle that's simple and worth repeating at home:

"School-approved does not automatically mean home-approved." — A Parent's Guide to Artificial Intelligence

This isn't about being more restrictive than the school. It's about understanding that the school's approval covers specific tools, in specific ways, with specific protections in place. Those conditions don't automatically carry over to a personal account at home. The school decisions don't necessarily reflect your values, or your families values. Your concerns about the safety, integrity, environmental impact, origin of AI training materials, or any other opinions you have are what should inform the decisions you make for you and your family.

A useful analogy: a school may approve field trips in school vehicles driven by certified drivers. That doesn't mean you'd let your child ride in any vehicle with anyone. The underlying activity (riding in a vehicle) is the same. The safety context is different.

We're not suggesting that home AI use is dangerous. Plenty of the tools in this series can be appropriate for family use, they just might not be right for your family. The decision about what's appropriate at home is yours to make — and making it deliberately is better than letting it happen by default.

What the ongoing debate looks like from a VSB parent's perspective

When the VSB announced Copilot 13+, not everyone was immediately in favour. The parent's guide notes that some students and parents raised concerns publicly, including:

  • Privacy concerns
  • Impact on independent thinking and learning
  • Environmental costs
  • Student safety

There was also a request for clearer policies and the option to opt out.3

These are reasonable concerns. If you share them, you're in good company — and you have the right to ask questions. The guide puts it clearly:

"If you have concerns, you are not alone, and you are entitled to raise them."

Families who want to limit or opt out of school-provided AI tools can contact the school or district directly. Asking is always the right move when you're unsure.

Questions worth asking your child's school

If you haven't already asked about AI at your child's school, here are some useful starting points:

  • Which AI tool is being used, and is it a school-managed account?
  • What data protections apply? Is there a privacy policy or acceptable use policy I can read?
  • Can my child opt out, and what does that look like in practice?
  • Are teachers trained to support students in using AI appropriately?
  • How is AI use explained to students in class?

You can find the VSB's current guidance at the VSB Digital Literacy and Use of AI in Education page and the VSB Copilot 13+ page. The parent's guide links to both of these in Appendix B.

It's also worth noting that the opinions on AI use may be very dynamic in your home. I am an AI skeptic, despite using and working with it every day. One of my children doesn't like AI technology at all and has strong opinions about it's use, even how I use it at work! My other child doesn't feel terribly strongly about it, and sees it as a useful tool for somethings, and a neat toy for others. These are important discussions to have with your children to help inform them of why you might make certain parenting choices, and to understand the impacts it has on you, your children, and their learning at school.

The short version:

  • Copilot 13+ (the school version) has data protections that the public home version doesn't automatically include.
  • "School-approved" doesn't automatically mean "home-approved" — the context is different.
  • Parents have the right to ask questions and request opt-out options.
  • Check the VSB's official guidance pages for current information, since policies are still evolving.
  • The questions to ask: What tool, what account type, what data protections, can we opt out?
