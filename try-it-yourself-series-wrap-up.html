Try It Yourself: What Twelve Posts About AI Taught Me About Parenting in the Age of AI

By Yvan Boily

Tags: series recap getting started critical thinking safety family

Series wrap-up — the Try It Yourself series, 12 posts, blog.parentsguidetoai.ca

When I started this series, I had one goal in mind: to help parents get past the point of reading about AI and into actually using it.

That might sound simple. But there's a meaningful gap between knowing that AI exists and knowing what it feels like to use it — and that gap matters when your child asks you about it, or when something goes wrong. The whole premise of the series was that experience changes how you have that conversation.

Twelve posts later, I want to look back at what we covered, what challenges came up along the way, and what I think the series actually achieved. If you read every post, this is a chance to see the bigger picture. If you're arriving here first, this is a map that might help you decide where to start.

What the series set out to do

The parent's guide at parentsguidetoai.ca covers the landscape of AI in B.C. schools and at home: what it is, the risks, the opportunities, the laws, and where to get help. It's designed to be a reference. You can read it once and come back to it.

This series was designed to do something different. Instead of explaining AI from the outside, it was built around hands-on exercises — things to actually try each week. The working assumption was that doing something builds a different kind of understanding than reading about it.

That assumption held up.

The twelve posts, in brief

Post 1 — Before You Talk to Your Kids About AI, Try It Yourself The series opener. The pitch: your kids are already using these tools, and you'll guide them better from experience than from theory. Three free tools to start with: ChatGPT, Copilot, and Google Gemini. The task: open one and ask a genuine question. That's it.

Post 2 — What AI Actually Is The one conceptual post in the series. AI works like a very sophisticated autocomplete — it predicts words, it doesn't think. This is why it can sound completely confident while being completely wrong. That gap between confidence and accuracy is the thing parents need to understand most.

Post 3 — A Plain-Language Comparison of the Five Main Tools ChatGPT, Copilot, Gemini, Claude, and Perplexity — what each one does, who made it, and which to try first. The short version: VSB families should start with Copilot; anyone who wants to verify information should try Perplexity. Check age minimums before letting children set up home accounts.

Post 4 — Your Privacy Checklist: What Never to Share With AI What you type into an AI tool doesn't always stay private. The post covers what to protect — passwords, home addresses, health information, photos, anything about classmates — and how to check the privacy settings on the tools you're using. The rule that applies to adults and children equally: never type anything you wouldn't want stored and shared.

Post 5 — How to Spot AI Mistakes Before Your Kids Don't AI mistakes look identical to correct answers. This post gives a simple four-question routine for catching errors: Where did this come from? Can I verify it? Does another source agree? Could this be fabricated? We also covered where mistakes are most likely — citations, statistics, names, dates, and anything specific to B.C. or recent events.

Post 6 — Using AI as Your Personal Research Assistant The counterbalance to all the caution. When AI is used for learning — summarizing documents, explaining concepts, breaking a complex topic into plain language — it is genuinely useful. Three practical tasks for parents: summarize a school communication, get a quick refresher on something your child is studying, and ask AI to explain your own job to a ten-year-old.

Post 7 — School AI vs. Home AI: What Every VSB Parent Should Understand Copilot 13+ at school and Copilot on your phone share a name and an underlying technology. They do not share the same protections. The school version includes managed accounts, commercial data protection, content filtering, and oversight. The public version has none of those guarantees by default. School-approved does not mean home-approved.

Post 8 — AI for Busy, Overwhelmed, or Differently-Abled Parents Everything the guide says about AI helping students with reading, writing, language barriers, and executive functioning also applies to parents. Four concrete applications: translation and language support, summarizing long school documents, planning and organization, and drafting communications you're struggling to write. This was one of the most practical posts in the series.

Post 9 — Deepfakes Are Real: What Parents Need to See to Understand The post that shifted tone. Deepfakes — AI-generated fake images, video, and audio of real people — are already being used to harm young people, including children in B.C. The technology is more accessible than most parents realize. The post also covered non-consensual intimate images, sextortion warning signs, and the specific B.C. resources available: takebackyourimages.gov.bc.ca, Cybertip.ca, and Kids Help Phone (1-800-668-6868).

Post 10 — Recognizing Bias in AI Answers AI produces the most statistically common answer — not the most accurate, not the most balanced, and not the one that best reflects your family's values or context. This post covered where AI bias comes from, what it looks like in practice, and three questions children (and adults) can ask to challenge any AI response: Whose perspective might be missing? Could this be unfair to certain groups? Would a Canadian or B.C.-specific source say something different?

Post 11 — Writing Your Family AI Agreement After ten posts building up your own understanding, this was the one designed to put it to use. A family AI agreement is not a list of rules handed down from a parent. It's a shared conversation that becomes a written reference. The post covered why agreements work better than rules, what to include, how to talk about it with different ages, and how to keep it from going stale as AI keeps changing.

Post 12 — Keeping Up When AI Changes Every Month The series closed with a practical resource list: the five pages every B.C. parent should bookmark. BC Ministry of Education's AI in Education page, the VSB's Digital Literacy guidance, MediaSmarts, the Office of the Privacy Commissioner, and Cybertip.ca. The goal wasn't to keep up with every development — it was to know where to look when something changes.

The challenges the series kept returning to

Looking at twelve posts together, a few tensions came up again and again. I want to name them directly.

Confidence without accuracy. This is the central problem with AI, and it ran through Posts 2, 5, and 10. AI does not know when it is wrong. It produces a response that sounds right regardless of whether it is. This isn't a bug that will be patched. It's a feature of how the technology works — and the only reliable response is a verification habit. Every post that involved checking something was, at its core, about this problem.

The school/home gap. Post 7 made the gap explicit, but it came up in almost every post. What children use at school is supervised, filtered, and governed by policy. What they access at home is not. Parents are the gap-filler. That's not a criticism — it's a reality of how AI has entered education, and the whole point of this series was to give parents the tools to fill that gap with knowledge rather than anxiety.

Safety is not a separate conversation. Post 9 sat in the middle of a series about practical skills, and that was deliberate. It would be easy to put deepfakes and sextortion in a separate, scarier category. But these are not hypothetical future risks — they are current harms affecting families in B.C. right now. They belong in the same conversation as privacy settings and the family agreement, not in a different one.

The goal was never to produce AI experts. This is worth saying plainly. The series was not trying to turn parents into AI power users. Every post was written to the question: what does a parent actually need to know to support their child and recognize when something has gone wrong? The answer to that question is much smaller than the internet makes it seem.

What the series couldn't cover

Twelve posts is not enough to cover everything. The standalone posts that follow this series go deeper on topics that come up as children get older, or as AI continues to change. That includes age-by-age guidance, academic integrity, AI scams targeting families, copyright and ownership, voice cloning, and how to talk to your child's school about AI policies.

Those posts are at blog.parentsguidetoai.ca. And the full guide — which covers everything in one document, with links to B.C.-specific resources — is always at parentsguidetoai.ca.

A personal note

I started writing the guide because people kept asking me for help — friends, colleagues, parents at school pickup who'd heard I worked in tech and wanted to know what they should do about AI. At some point it made more sense to write it all down than to keep having the same conversation.

What I didn't fully anticipate was how much my own parenting would get tangled up in it along the way. The guide started as an answer to other people's questions. The blog series started because answering those questions made clear that reading about AI and using it are very different things — and that most parents hadn't done the second one.

I still believe that the most useful thing you can do is use one of these tools yourself, just once, for five minutes. Not to become an expert. Not to have all the answers. Just to stop being someone who's only read about it.

If you did that — if you opened ChatGPT or Copilot or Gemini and asked a question somewhere along the way — this series did what it set out to do.

The short version:

  • The series ran twelve posts from June 12 to June 23, covering AI basics, privacy, verification, school vs. home, accessibility, deepfakes, bias, the family agreement, and how to stay current.
  • The central challenge throughout: AI sounds confident whether or not it's right. The response to that is a verification habit, not avoidance.
  • The school/home gap is real. Parents are the bridge. This series was designed to help with that.
  • Safety topics (deepfakes, sextortion) belong in the same conversation as the practical skills — not in a separate, scarier one.
  • The full guide is at parentsguidetoai.ca. The standalone posts continue at blog.parentsguidetoai.ca.

Try it today: Go back to the post in this series that you skipped or meant to come back to. If you read every one, open the parent's guide at parentsguidetoai.ca and read one section you haven't looked at closely. The guide and the series are designed to work together — each one fills in what the other leaves out.
