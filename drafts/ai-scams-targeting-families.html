AI Scams Targeting Families: Voice Cloning, Fake Emergencies, and What to Do

By Yvan Boily

Tags: scams voice cloning fraud grandparent scam phishing family safety

Most of the conversations in this blog and in the parent's guide focus on how AI affects children. This post is about how AI is being used to target families — including the adults in them.

AI-powered scams targeting families are not theoretical. They are happening now, in Canada, including in BC. They have successfully defrauded real people out of real money, and they have caused significant distress to families who discovered that the panicked call from their "child" was staged by a machine.

This is worth understanding before it happens to you.

What voice cloning actually is

Modern AI systems can clone a person's voice from a short audio sample — in some cases, as little as three to five seconds of recorded speech.1 The cloned voice can then say anything.

The output is not a recording. It is a generated audio file that sounds like the person speaking words they never said. The quality varies, but in casual phone-call conditions — not side-by-side comparison with the real person — it can be convincing enough to deceive people who know the target's voice well.2

The audio samples needed to create a clone are available for almost any teenager or adult who has posted videos on social media, left voicemails, or appeared in video calls. Your child's voice may already exist in enough publicly available material to clone.

The grandparent scam, AI-powered

The "grandparent scam" has existed for years: a caller impersonates a grandchild in distress ("Grandma, I'm in trouble, don't tell Mom and Dad") and asks for money urgently, often through wire transfer or gift cards.

AI voice cloning has made this scam significantly more effective.

A scammer can now call a grandparent using a voice that sounds like their actual grandchild, not a generic young voice. They can combine the voice clone with information gathered from social media — the grandchild's name, details about their life, recent events — to add convincing detail. The caller may then hand the phone to a fake "lawyer" or "police officer" who explains what the grandchild needs.

This works because the emotional urgency of the call makes careful evaluation harder. A grandparent who hears their grandchild's voice and genuine-sounding distress activates a protective response, not a fraud-detection response.

The Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre has documented these calls in Canada, including cases where amounts in the tens of thousands of dollars were transferred before the fraud was discovered.3

The fake emergency call

A related scam targets parents directly. A caller impersonates a child's voice to say they've been in an accident, arrested, or kidnapped. In the kidnapping variation — sometimes called a "virtual kidnapping" scam — parents hear the child screaming or crying, and then the call is taken over by a scammer making demands.

These calls are designed to be short and panicked, to prevent the target from hanging up and calling back. The scammer keeps the target on the line, building emotional urgency while a second scammer instructs them on transferring money.

The detail that makes AI voice cloning particularly effective here is that even a parent who is initially skeptical may be convinced when the voice sounds genuinely like their child. The threshold for "this might be real" goes up when the voice is familiar.

Phishing, impersonation, and AI-generated text

Voice cloning is the most dramatic example, but AI is also being used to improve traditional scams that target families.

AI-generated phishing messages are harder to detect than older phishing. Historically, phishing emails contained spelling errors and grammatical mistakes. AI-generated phishing produces grammatically correct, contextually appropriate messages that can be personalized to the target — referencing their school, their employer, their neighbourhood.

Impersonation of school officials or school boards is a documented scam type. A message that appears to be from your child's principal or from the school district, with appropriate official language, requesting payment for a trip deposit or explaining that your child is in a situation requiring your immediate attention.

AI-generated fake invoices and utility notices increasingly target families. A fake notice from a recognizable utility or government agency — with appropriate formatting and language — is harder to dismiss than older scams.

How to protect your family

Establish a code word

This is the most effective tool against voice cloning scams. Agree on a word or phrase — something specific that only family members know — that can be used in a call to verify identity. A person in genuine distress can say the code word. An AI clone cannot, because it doesn't know it.

Establish this with your children, your parents, and anyone who might receive a distress call related to your family. Review it periodically in case it's forgotten.

Call back on a known number

If you receive a call claiming your child is in trouble, hang up and call your child's actual phone. Call the school. Call the hospital or police station the caller mentioned and verify the situation independently. A real emergency will survive a two-minute verification call. A scam will not.

Do not call back the number that called you. Use a number you already have.

Slow down on any request for money

The common denominator in almost every AI-assisted scam is urgency — "act now, don't tell anyone, the window will close." This urgency is a manipulation tool. Any legitimate emergency involving your family can withstand you saying "I need to make one call to verify this before I do anything."

Scammers will push back on this. A legitimate caller will not.

Limit the audio samples available publicly

If your child appears in videos on social media — their own account or others' — those videos provide voice samples that could theoretically be used for cloning. This is not a reason to eliminate your child's social media presence, but it is worth factoring into decisions about what gets posted.

Private accounts reduce but don't eliminate this risk. Content shared in private messages can be screenshotted or recorded.

Talk to grandparents and older relatives

Older adults are disproportionately targeted by grandparent scams. If your children have grandparents or older relatives, a direct conversation about this scam type — including what the code word is — is worth having. This is not about doubting their judgment. It's about giving them a tool they don't have without the conversation.

If you've been targeted

If you or a family member receives a suspicious call like the ones described here:

  • Do not send money until you have independently verified the situation.
  • Report to the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre — antifraudcentre.ca or call 1-888-495-8501. Even if no money was lost, reporting helps track these scams and can prevent others from being victimized.
  • Report to local police if money was transferred.
  • Contact your bank immediately if a wire transfer or e-transfer was made — there may be a short window to recall it.

If you or a family member is distressed after a scam call — even if it didn't result in loss — this is a legitimate emotional response. Kids Help Phone (1-800-668-6868) is for young people. Crisis Centre BC (1-800-784-2433) serves adults.

Having the conversation with your family

The goal of this post is not to generate anxiety about every phone call. It's to give your family tools they can use before they're in a stressful situation.

Consider raising this at your next family meal, or when you're next in the car together:

  • "I've been reading about a scam where someone clones your voice using AI to trick your grandparents. I want to set up a code word we can all use."
  • "If you got a call that sounded like me but felt weird, what would you do?"
  • "What would make you suspicious that a phone call wasn't what it seemed?"

The most effective protection is a family that has talked about this before it happens — and has a specific, shared tool (the code word) they can use in the moment.

The short version:

  • AI can clone a person's voice from a short audio sample. Scammers are using this to impersonate children in distress and deceive grandparents or parents into sending money.
  • The key tactics are urgency and emotional pressure. A real emergency can survive a two-minute verification call. A scam cannot.
  • Establish a code word with your family — something only real family members know — before you need it.
  • Call back on a known number. Never call back the number that called you.
  • Report to the Canadian Anti-Fraud Centre: antifraudcentre.ca or 1-888-495-8501.
