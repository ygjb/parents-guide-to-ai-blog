Talking to Your School About AI: Questions to Ask, Rights to Know, and How to Opt Out

By Yvan Boily

Tags: schools VSB opt-out parents policy rights Copilot 13+

The parent's guide at parentsguidetoai.ca is clear on one point: parents are entitled to ask questions about AI in their child's school, and different families will reach different conclusions about AI, and that is okay.

But knowing you're entitled to ask is different from knowing what to ask. This post is a practical guide to the conversations worth having with your child's school — whether you're broadly supportive of AI in education, deeply skeptical, or somewhere in between.

Why these conversations matter

AI tools in schools are not neutral additions to the curriculum. They involve:

  • Student data: what your child types, how they interact, potentially what they produce.
  • Vendor relationships: school boards sign contracts with technology companies on behalf of students and families.
  • Pedagogical choices: decisions about what skills AI should develop versus what skills it might replace.
  • Equity implications: students who are confident technology users may navigate AI tools differently from those who aren't.
  • Safety and content: questions about what content filtering is in place and how it works.

You are not overreaching by asking about these things. You are doing exactly what a parent should do.

The right to ask and the right to information

Under BC's Freedom of Information and Protection of Privacy Act (FIPPA), parents have rights to information about how their children's personal information is collected and used by public bodies, including school boards. School districts are required to have privacy officers and acceptable use policies.1

You can:

  • Request a copy of your school district's privacy policy and acceptable use policy for AI tools.
  • Ask how your child's data is handled when they use a school-provided AI tool.
  • Ask who has access to your child's interactions with school AI tools.
  • Ask what happens to your child's data if the contract with the AI vendor ends.

Most districts will answer these questions directly if you ask. If you encounter difficulty, you can escalate to the district's privacy officer or, if necessary, the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner of BC.

Questions to ask your child's school

Here are specific questions worth raising with teachers, principals, or district administration. You don't need to ask all of them — pick the ones most relevant to your situation.

About the tools themselves

  • "What AI tools does the school or district have approved for student use?"
  • "Which of these tools will my child use this year, and for what purposes?"
  • "For the VSB's Copilot 13+: what protections are in place for student data, and are student conversations used to train public AI models?"
  • "What content filtering is in place? How is it monitored and updated?"
  • "Has the school or district completed a Privacy Impact Assessment for AI tools used in classrooms?"2

About data and privacy

  • "Where is my child's data stored, and in which country?"3
  • "What does the vendor's data retention policy look like? How long is student data kept?"
  • "Is my child's data ever shared with third parties? Under what circumstances?"
  • "What would happen to data already collected if the school stopped using the AI tool?"

About how AI is used in teaching

  • "How are teachers using AI in this class? Is AI use explained to students?"
  • "What is the school's position on students using AI for assignments? Does this vary by teacher or course?"
  • "Is there specific AI literacy teaching — explaining what AI is and how to use it critically?"

About consent and options

  • "Do I need to provide consent for my child to use school AI tools?"
  • "Can my child opt out of using AI tools at school? What does that process look like?"
  • "If my child opts out, will they be at any academic disadvantage?"

How to opt out — and what "opting out" means in practice

The parent's guide states clearly:

"Families who wish to limit or opt out of their child's use of school-provided AI tools should contact their child's school or district directly to ask what options are available."

Opting out is a legitimate choice. It also has practical implications worth understanding.

What opt-out typically means: In most cases, opting out means your child's school-managed AI account is not activated, or the AI features are disabled on their school account. This does not necessarily prevent your child from being in a classroom where other students are using AI, or from assignments that incorporate AI as a class-level activity.

What opt-out does not necessarily mean: It does not automatically extend to every AI-powered tool in the school environment. Some tools that are AI-powered are not typically understood as "AI tools" — digital textbooks with adaptive features, school library databases with AI search, accessibility tools. Clarifying the scope of your opt-out request is worth doing explicitly.

The conversation with your child: If you choose to opt out, your child will benefit from understanding why — and from not feeling disadvantaged or singled out. Framing the decision as a family value choice, rather than a punitive measure, is more likely to lead to a constructive outcome.

Review your decision annually: AI tools in schools are changing quickly. A decision that was right for your family in September 2026 may look different in September 2027. Building in a yearly review — to reassess what's being used and whether your position has changed — is reasonable.

If you're raising concerns, not opting out

Not every parent who has concerns about AI in schools wants to opt out. You may want to advocate for clearer policies, stronger privacy protections, or more transparent communication from the district — without removing your child from AI tools entirely.

In this case, the following routes are available:

Talk to the teacher first. Teachers are the most direct contact for classroom-level AI use. A respectful conversation — "I wanted to understand how AI tools are being used in your class before the school year is underway" — is a good starting point.

Contact the school principal. For school-level policy questions or concerns about how a particular situation was handled.

Engage through your Parent Advisory Council (PAC). PACs have formal standing to engage with school administration on educational policy questions. Raising AI as a PAC agenda item is a way to ensure that parent perspectives are part of the conversation at the school level.

Engage with the school district. For VSB families: the Vancouver School Board has public meetings, a trustee contact system, and communications channels for parent feedback. If you have policy-level concerns about the Copilot 13+ rollout or other district-wide AI decisions, the district is the right level to engage.

File a complaint with the Office of the Information and Privacy Commissioner of BC if you believe your child's personal information is being handled in a way that violates FIPPA. oipc.bc.ca

Being part of the solution

One thing worth holding onto: schools are not adversaries in this conversation. Most educators are navigating AI with the same uncertainty parents are — trying to make decisions that serve students with incomplete information about a technology that is changing faster than institutional processes can keep up with.

Showing up as a curious, engaged parent who wants to understand what's happening — rather than arriving with an adversarial posture — tends to produce better conversations and better outcomes. The teachers and principals who are most thoughtful about AI are often the ones who want more parent engagement, not less.

The parent's guide puts it well:

"Asking questions is part of responsible digital citizenship, and different families may reach different conclusions about AI, and that is okay."

Your school needs to hear those questions. Ask them.

The short version:

  • You have the right to ask what AI tools your child is using, how their data is protected, and whether you can opt out.
  • Under FIPPA, BC school districts are required to have privacy policies and to respond to questions about student data.
  • Opting out is a legitimate choice with practical implications — clarify the scope with your school and review the decision annually.
  • If you have concerns short of opting out: talk to the teacher, engage your PAC, or contact the district.
  • Approach the conversation as a curious, engaged parent — most teachers navigating AI want more parent engagement, not less.
