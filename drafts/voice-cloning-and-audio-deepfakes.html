Audio Deepfakes and Voice Cloning: The Deepfake You Can't See

By Yvan Boily

Tags: deepfakes voice cloning audio podcast phone calls scams misinformation

Post 9 in this series introduced deepfakes — AI-generated fake media — and focused primarily on fake images and video, which are the most visible form of this problem. Post 14 covered the situation where a child is the one creating them.

This post covers a specific type of deepfake that deserves its own treatment: audio deepfakes — AI-generated fake voice recordings and phone calls. Audio deepfakes are in some ways harder to detect than image or video fakes, and they are already being used in ways that directly affect families.

What voice cloning actually produces

Modern AI voice cloning systems can generate a convincing imitation of a person's voice using as little as three to ten seconds of audio.1 The output is not a recording — it is a generated audio file where the AI predicts what the target person would sound like saying specific words, based on patterns learned from the sample.

The quality depends on the length and quality of the sample, the skill of the system, and the listening conditions. In a quiet room with good speakers, a trained listener may detect subtle artifacts. On a phone call with typical compression and background noise, the same audio may be indistinguishable from the real person.2

Audio samples for cloning are everywhere. Most teenagers and adults who have spoken publicly online — in TikTok videos, YouTube videos, Reels, podcasts, video calls, voicemails, or any other format — have provided enough audio for a voice clone. The samples don't need to be long, and they don't need to be high quality.

How audio deepfakes are being used to harm young people

Fake audio of teens shared in school communities

This is the specific harm pattern that concerns me most for families reading this blog.

A student with a voice sample of a classmate — taken from a video posted to any social platform — can use available tools to generate fake audio of that classmate saying things they never said: slurs, explicit statements, confessions of things they didn't do, expressions of romantic or sexual interest they never expressed.

This audio can then be shared in group chats and social networks as if it were real. The damage to the target's reputation can happen before anyone has a chance to verify that it's fake.

This is not a theoretical risk. Audio deepfakes have been used in documented bullying cases in the United States and elsewhere. The harm is real, and the tools to do it are widely available.3

Parental voice impersonation

Scenarios where AI-generated audio impersonates a parent, guardian, or authority figure are emerging. A fake audio clip of a parent "approving" something, or of a school official saying something they never said, can be used to manipulate other adults or the child themselves.

Teacher and school authority impersonation

Similar to the above: fake audio of a teacher making inappropriate comments, or of school administration conveying false information, has been reported in other jurisdictions. This is not yet a common pattern in Canada, but it is a documented pattern elsewhere that is worth knowing about.

Sextortion with audio

Post 9 covered sextortion — coercive threats involving intimate images. The same pattern can involve audio: fake audio clips purporting to depict a person saying sexual things, or engaging in a sexual conversation, used as leverage. As with image-based sextortion, the content doesn't need to be real to be used as a threat.

Why audio deepfakes are harder to detect

With image and video deepfakes, detection has become a significant research area. Forensic tools examine pixel-level artifacts, facial movement inconsistencies, and lighting irregularities. While detection is not foolproof, there is an established field working on it.

Audio deepfakes are harder to detect for several reasons:

Phone calls compress and degrade audio naturally. The artifacts that would betray a voice clone in studio conditions may be masked by the normal degradation of a phone call.

Emotional state affects perception. Research on voice cloning and deception consistently finds that people are worse at detecting fake voices when they are emotionally aroused — anxious, upset, frightened. This is exactly the state in which voice cloning scams are designed to catch people.

There are no universal visual cues. With video deepfakes, there are sometimes visual signs — unnatural blinking, inconsistent lighting, edge artifacts. With audio, the absence of visual cues removes these secondary checks.

Short clips are more convincing than long ones. A five-second clip of a familiar voice saying one sentence is harder to evaluate critically than a ten-minute conversation. Many audio deepfake attacks use short clips.

Talking to your child about audio deepfakes

This conversation has some unique dimensions compared to the image deepfake conversation.

Your child's voice is already available for cloning. If they've posted videos, this is true. This doesn't mean they need to delete their presence online — it means they should know it's true.

Fake audio of your child could be used to harm them. The bullying use case described above is real. Your child should know that if they hear audio that sounds like them saying something they didn't say, or if such audio is shared about them, it may be an audio deepfake — and this is something they can report.

Your child might use audio tools without understanding the implications. AI voice tools are widely available and sometimes marketed as entertainment ("make your voice sound like a celebrity"). The line between a funny tool and a harmful one is crossed when the generated audio is used against a real person without their consent.

Verifying a voice on a call is now a legitimate skill. The code word approach described in Post 18 on AI scams applies directly to audio deepfakes. A code word that only real family members know is a verification tool that doesn't depend on being able to detect deepfakes technically.

What to do if audio deepfakes target your child or family

If fake audio of your child is being shared at school: Follow the steps from Post 9. Document the audio (screenshot or recording of where it's being shared), preserve evidence, and contact the school. If it involves intimate content, use Take Back Your Images and Cybertip.ca. Use ERASE to report to the school's safe-school coordinator.

If fake audio is being used for sextortion: See Post 9 for the full response protocol. The key points: do not pay, do not send more images or audio, preserve evidence, and report to Cybertip.ca and police.

If you receive a suspicious phone call: See Post 18 on AI scams. Hang up and call back on a known number. Use your code word. Do not send money before independently verifying the situation.

If fake audio is being spread as misinformation: The same critical thinking approach from Post 5 applies to audio: find the original source, look for independent verification, and be suspicious of audio that is convenient for one side of a story.

A note on audio deepfakes and misinformation

Beyond the personal harms, audio deepfakes are increasingly used for political and social misinformation. Fake audio of politicians, public figures, and community leaders saying things they never said has been used in multiple countries to influence public opinion.4

This is worth discussing with older teenagers especially — the same critical thinking skills that apply to images apply to audio, but the habit of skepticism about audio has not been widely cultivated. We're used to hearing "I'll believe it when I see it." The audio deepfake era calls for "I'll believe it when I've verified it."

The short version:

  • AI can clone a voice from seconds of audio and generate convincing fake recordings. Most teenagers with a social media presence have already provided enough audio for a voice clone.
  • Audio deepfakes are being used for school-level bullying, scams, and sextortion. They are harder to detect than image fakes because phone audio is naturally degraded.
  • A code word known only to family members is a practical, low-tech defense against voice cloning scams.
  • If fake audio of your child is being shared: document, preserve evidence, contact school, use ERASE and Cybertip.ca.
  • Apply the same skepticism to audio that you've learned to apply to images: convenient audio clips that "prove" something are worth verifying independently.
