Part 8 - AI for Busy, Overwhelmed, or Differently-Abled Parents

By Yvan Boily

Tags: accessibility translation summarization organization

Part 8 of 12 in the Try It Yourself series — practical AI guidance for parents at blog.parentsguidetoai.ca

The parent's guide at parentsguidetoai.ca has a section on how AI can help students who face challenges with reading, writing, language barriers, organization, or executive functioning. It's one of the most practical parts of the guide.

What it doesn't say explicitly — but what's worth saying here — is that every one of those challenges also applies to parents.

Parenting is time-compressed. School communications arrive in dense documents at inconvenient moments. Many parents are navigating systems in a language that isn't their first. Many live with ADHD, dyslexia, or other conditions that make reading long text or managing competing priorities genuinely hard.

AI doesn't solve all of that. But it helps more than most people realize. This post covers four concrete applications for parents.

Translation and language support

If English is not your primary language, communicating with schools can be frustrating. Official documents are often written in formal, institutional language — the kind that's hard even for native speakers.

Google Gemini and ChatGPT both handle translation well.1 You can paste a document in English and ask for a translation into another language. You can also ask for the explanation of a concept in your preferred language, and then read about it in the language that makes the most sense to you.

Try a prompt like:

"Please translate this school newsletter into [your language], using plain everyday language."

Then paste the document text.

I'll use a personal example here, I grew up in a bilingual (English primary, and French) household, and took French immersion classes as a child, but I haven't regularly written or spoken French in many years. My children are in French immersion, and while my French has come back over the years, I still make use of AI translation tools when there are assignments that use scientific language that doesn't easily translate, or when there are complex word problems in math or other assignments.

You can also use AI in the other direction — drafting a message to a teacher in English when that's not your first language. Type your thoughts in your own language, then ask AI to help you write a clear email in English. Read it carefully before you send it to make sure it says what you intended.

Summarization for the time-crunched parent

Most school communications are longer than they need to be. Policy updates, newsletter updates, field trip information packages, acceptable use agreements — the average school parent reads a significant amount of administrative text every year.

AI summarization is genuinely useful here. Paste any document into ChatGPT, Copilot, or Claude and ask:

"Summarize this in five bullet points. Include any action items that require my response."

The response will be faster and shorter than reading the full document. Check it against the original for anything important — AI summaries occasionally drop key details. But for a first pass on a long document, it's a real time-saver.

One particularly useful application: the parent's guide itself. The full guide is thorough and well-organized, but it's also long. If there's a section you want to understand quickly — the deepfakes section, the privacy section, the section on Copilot 13+ — paste it into an AI tool and ask for a plain-language summary. Then go back to the original for anything that needs more detail.

Planning, organizing, and drafting

For parents who find organization challenging — including those managing ADHD or other executive functioning difficulties — AI can help with the parts of parenting admin that involve getting information out of your head and into an organized form.

Some practical examples:

Drafting emails to teachers or schools. Tell AI what you want to communicate, and ask it to help you write a clear, professional email. You provide the substance; AI helps with the structure and wording.

Creating a weekly schedule. Describe your child's activities, school times, and other commitments, and ask AI to help you organize them into a weekly plan.

Preparing for parent-teacher meetings. As we covered in Post 6, AI is useful for generating questions ahead of a meeting. It's also useful for organizing the notes you take during one.

Breaking down a complex task. If you're trying to figure out how to handle a situation at school — a conflict, a concern about your child's learning, a question about policy — you can talk through the situation with AI and ask it to help you identify next steps.

One way that I use AI is to help manage and coordinate schedules. I use AI tools like Gemini that is built into my Google account to find emails for kids sports and events and make sure they are all added to my calendar, and scan emails related to them to make sure I have followed up on any requests or actions. This is especially important for teams and activities where my wife or I are organizers or have obligations (such as being safety or score keeper for my daughters hockey team).

AI writing tools built into things you already use

You may already have AI assistance available in tools you use every day without realizing it.

If you use Google Docs, Gemini AI is integrated and can help you draft, rewrite, or summarize directly in the document. Look for the Gemini icon in the toolbar.2

If you use Microsoft Word or Outlook, Copilot is available in many versions and can help with writing and summarization. Look for the Copilot icon in the app.3

These are worth knowing about because they don't require you to open a separate tool. The assistance is available where you're already working.

As always: don't paste private information about your child or other people into these tools, and review everything AI helps you write before you send or publish it.

The short version:

  • AI's accessibility features aren't just for students — they're useful for parents too.
  • Translation: paste school documents and ask for a summary in your preferred language.
  • Summarization: get the key points from long school communications faster.
  • Planning and drafting: AI can help structure your thoughts into emails, schedules, and meeting prep.
  • Google Docs and Microsoft Word have AI features built in — you may already have access.

We've covered a lot of practical ground. The next post shifts tone a bit. We're going to look at one of the more serious risks the parent's guide covers: deepfakes. Understanding this isn't optional for parents right now.

Try it today: Paste the last long document you received from your child's school into ChatGPT or Copilot and ask for a five-bullet summary. Note what it captures well and what it misses.
