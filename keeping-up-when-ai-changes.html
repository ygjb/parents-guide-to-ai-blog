Part 12 - Keeping Up When AI Changes Every Month: Five Resources Every Parent Should Bookmark

By Yvan Boily

Tags: resources staying current BC MediaSmarts Cybertip

Part 12 of 12 in the Try It Yourself series — practical AI guidance for parents at blog.parentsguidetoai.ca

If there's one honest thing to say about AI right now, it's this: what's true today may not be true in six months.

Tools change. School policies update. New harms emerge. Legislation evolves. The parent's guide itself includes this warning:

"AI tools, school policies, and support laws change quickly."

That's not a reason to be anxious. It's a reason to have a short list of reliable places to check — so you can stay informed without spending more time on it than you have.

This final post gives you five resources to bookmark. They're maintained by organizations that exist to track exactly this kind of change, and they're appropriate for BC parents specifically.

The five resources

1. BC Ministry of Education — AI in Education

https://www2.gov.bc.ca/gov/content/education-training/k-12/administration/program-management/ai-in-education

The provincial government's page on AI in K-12 education. This is where BC-wide policy and guidance for schools is published. If there's a province-wide change to how AI is handled in schools, it will appear here.

Check this page at the start of each school year, and any time you hear about a significant policy change.

2. Vancouver School Board — Digital Literacy and Copilot 13+

VSB Digital Literacy and Use of AI in Education VSB Copilot 13+ page

The VSB's official guidance for parents and students. This is where the Vancouver School Board explains its current approach to AI, including the Copilot 13+ rollout covered in Post 7.

Policies at the district level can change more quickly than provincial policies. If something changes with Copilot 13+, or if the VSB updates its acceptable use guidelines, these pages will reflect it.

3. MediaSmarts — Parent Resources on AI and Algorithms

https://mediasmarts.ca

MediaSmarts is Canada's centre for digital and media literacy. It produces research, guides, and lesson materials specifically for Canadian families and educators. Its resources are consistently well-sourced, written in plain language, and updated as the landscape changes.

Their parent resources section includes guides on AI, algorithms, social media, and online safety. This is the most consistently useful general resource on this list for Canadian parents.

4. Office of the Privacy Commissioner of Canada — Privacy and AI

https://www.priv.gc.ca/en/privacy-topics/technology/artificial-intelligence/

The OPC is Canada's federal privacy regulator. Its website includes guidance on AI and privacy that's relevant to both children and adults — including resources on what AI companies are and aren't permitted to do with Canadian users' data.

This is the right place to look if you want to understand your privacy rights in relation to AI tools, or if you're following regulatory developments around AI and data protection.

5. Cybertip.ca and Kids Help Phone

https://www.cybertip.ca — Report online child sexual exploitation material, including AI-generated content involving minors.

Kids Help Phone — 1-800-668-6868 (call 24/7), or text CONNECT to 686868.

These aren't resources for staying current on AI news — they're for when something goes wrong. We covered them in Post 9. They're here again because they're worth having bookmarked before you need them, not after.

Using AI to stay current on AI

One practical approach for busy parents: use a tool like Perplexity to search for recent news. Perplexity surfaces current articles with sources, so you can quickly scan what's changed without doing a full news search.

Try a prompt like:

"What's changed in Canada regarding AI and children in schools in the last three months?"

Or:

"Are there any recent updates to VSB AI policy for 2026?"

Check the sources it cites. If they're from reliable outlets, take the summary as a useful starting point and click through to verify anything important.

The habits that outlast any specific tool

We covered twelve topics in this series. The specific tools and policies will change. These habits won't.

  1. Try AI before you form a strong opinion about it.
  2. Verify before you trust — citations, statistics, local information especially.
  3. Check privacy settings when you start using any new tool.
  4. Remember that "school-approved" and "home-approved" are different things.
  5. Treat AI conversations as semi-public.
  6. Ask whose perspective is reflected in an AI answer — and what's missing.
  7. Use AI to understand, not just to produce.
  8. Know where to report something if something goes wrong.
  9. Have the family AI conversation before you need to, not after.
  10. Write down your family expectations, even briefly.
  11. Review those expectations at the start of each school year.
  12. You don't need to become a technical expert. You need to stay curious and engaged.

The parent's guide puts the last point this way:

"Parents don't need to become technical experts to guide their children through the AI era. They need to stay curious, stay engaged, and create space for honest conversations — the same things that have always made the difference."

That's the goal this series was working toward. Not mastery. Confidence.

Thank you for following along.
